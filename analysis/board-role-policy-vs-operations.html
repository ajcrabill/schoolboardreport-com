Board Role: Governance vs. Operations — The Line That Keeps Moving

June 2, 2026|School Board Report Analysis
Back to Analysis

Every school board governance crisis has the same fingerprints: a board that stopped asking where the line is and started treating that line as a suggestion. Understanding why boards drift into operational territory — and why that drift almost always damages student outcomes — is the foundational question of effective governance.

The Line Is Real, Even When It's Invisible

Ask most school board members to describe the boundary between governance and operations and you'll get a confident answer that unravels under scrutiny. "We set policy; staff implement it" sounds clean, but it leaves every contested question exactly where it started. Who decides how a policy is interpreted when two administrators disagree? Who determines whether implementation is actually happening? When a constituent calls a board member at 9 p.m. to complain that their child's teacher isn't following district policy, what exactly is the board member supposed to do?

The governance-operations boundary is not an abstract philosophical distinction — it has concrete, practical consequences for who makes decisions, who is accountable for outcomes, and whether the system as a whole can learn from its mistakes. When that boundary blurs, accountability blurs with it. Superintendents stop owning outcomes because they're never fully in control of the means. Board members stop owning governance because they're too busy managing. And students end up in a system where no one is clearly responsible for anything.

How Boards Drift — and Why It Feels Justified

Operational drift rarely happens because board members are malicious or ignorant. It happens because individual cases are compelling, constituents are present, and the stakes feel real in ways that abstract governance discussions do not. A board member who sits through an hour of student performance data might feel genuinely helpless. But a board member who corners the curriculum director after the meeting to insist on a specific intervention for a struggling school — that board member feels like they're doing something.

That feeling is the trap. It confuses motion for governance. The board member who demands that staff hire a particular reading coach, approve a specific vendor contract because a constituent recommended it, or publicly second-guess a principal's disciplinary decision is not governing. They're administering — and doing it badly, because they lack the operational context that comes from being in the building every day. The superintendent, meanwhile, is left trying to manage a system whose leadership structure just fractured in real time.

"When a board directs staff around the superintendent, it doesn't just undermine one administrator — it tells every employee that the actual chain of authority is optional, subject to revision by whoever shows up loudest at the next meeting."

The Superintendent as the Hinge

The superintendent occupies an unusual position in this dynamic: they are simultaneously the board's most important employee and the person most damaged when the board behaves operationally. Every time a board member contacts a principal directly to resolve a constituent complaint, the superintendent's authority erodes a little. Every time the board approves a personnel decision that should have been a staff recommendation, the message to the organization is that the formal hierarchy is provisional.

Effective boards protect superintendent authority not out of deference, but because organizational coherence depends on it. A superintendent who cannot make staffing decisions without board interference cannot be held accountable for staffing outcomes. A board that wants to evaluate superintendent performance against clear goals must first give that superintendent enough control over operations to actually drive those goals. Operational interference and meaningful accountability cannot coexist — they cancel each other out.

Policy as the Proper Channel

The most durable solution to governance drift is a board that has genuinely internalized what policy authority actually enables. Policy is not a consolation prize for boards that can't touch operations — it is a more powerful lever than any single operational intervention. A board that sets a rigorous policy on early literacy, with explicit outcome targets and a defined monitoring cadence, will do more for reading achievement than a board member who hand-picks a phonics curriculum. The policy creates system-wide expectations. The operational interference creates a one-time workaround that may not even survive the next budget cycle.

This reframe matters because many board members arrive with genuine expertise — in finance, healthcare, law, community organizing — and feel underutilized when they're told to stay out of operations. The answer is not to neuter that expertise but to redirect it. A board member with financial acumen is invaluable when the board is setting fiscal policy, evaluating budget frameworks, or probing whether the superintendent's resource allocation aligns with adopted priorities. That same expertise becomes disruptive when it's applied to questioning individual procurement decisions that should have already been governed by board-approved policy.

Warning Signs That the Line Has Already Moved

Boards that have drifted operationally rarely announce it. The warning signs are behavioral: board members who regularly attend staff meetings without a governance purpose; agendas dominated by operational approvals that have no policy dimension; consent calendars that are consistently pulled for individual discussion; board members who can name the principals in every school but cannot articulate the board's current student outcome goals. Perhaps most tellingly: a superintendent who has stopped bringing the board difficult strategic choices because it's easier to just make those choices quietly than to endure the operational commentary that follows.

When a board reaches that last condition — where the superintendent withholds strategic information to avoid the board's operational instincts — the governance relationship has effectively failed. The board has trained its own chief executive to work around it. Rebuilding that relationship requires both the board and superintendent to name the dynamic explicitly and recommit to the roles that make the system functional: the board governing through policy and goal-setting, the superintendent operating through execution and transparent reporting.

Governance Discipline as a Student Outcome Strategy

The districts that sustain long-term improvement in student outcomes share a common structural feature: a board that has made a durable commitment to governing rather than operating, and a superintendent who is genuinely accountable to outcome goals rather than to the board's shifting operational preferences. That combination — clear board goals, protected superintendent authority, rigorous monitoring — is not a governance style choice. It is a precondition for the kind of organizational learning that produces sustained academic gains.

The line between governance and operations keeps moving in most districts because there is no institutional cost to moving it. Board members face no accountability for operational interference, and superintendents face accountability for outcomes they do not control. Fixing that requires boards to be honest about what good governance actually demands of them — not less engagement with the work of education, but a different kind of engagement, one that is harder to see on a single agenda item but far more consequential across years and across the students those decisions ultimately serve.
