--- layout: article title: "Book Bans and Board Authority: Where Governance Ends and Politics Begin" category: "Curriculum and Policy" date_display: "June 16, 2026" ---

When school boards vote to remove books from library shelves, they often frame it as a curriculum decision — a routine exercise of board authority over instructional materials. But the pattern of how these decisions get made, who drives them, and what processes get bypassed reveals something more troubling: boards routinely substituting political performance for governance discipline, and in doing so, undermining the very institutional credibility they depend on to function.

The Governance Question Nobody Is Asking

The public debate over book bans tends to collapse immediately into a culture war, with advocates on each side treating the question as self-evidently about values — either protecting children or protecting intellectual freedom. What gets lost is a prior question that boards should be answering first: is this decision even within the appropriate scope of board action, and if so, what process should govern it?

School boards are not literary critics. They are not child psychologists, curriculum experts, or constitutional scholars. They are governing bodies charged with setting policy direction, holding the superintendent accountable for results, and stewarding public resources. The moment a board starts convening emergency sessions to debate the content of individual novels — rather than reviewing whether the district's materials-selection policy is sound and consistently applied — it has left governance and entered management. That's a boundary violation with real costs, regardless of which titles are at issue.

Policy Versus Politics: A Distinction Boards Keep Blurring

There is a legitimate board role in this space. Boards can and should set a clear, principled policy for how instructional materials are selected, challenged, reviewed, and ultimately retained or removed. That policy should define who has standing to challenge a title, what professional review process applies, what criteria govern decisions, and how the board receives final recommendations. This is governance work — establishing systems and holding staff accountable for operating them faithfully.

What boards across the country have done instead is short-circuit that process entirely. In Llano County, Texas, a county judge found in 2023 that board members had removed books specifically because they personally disagreed with their content, without any reference to the district's established review procedures. In multiple Florida districts following the state's expansive 2022 book-challenge law, boards voted on removal lists compiled by a single parent complainant with no professional review whatsoever. The pattern is consistent: when political pressure is high enough, boards abandon their own policies — the same policies they put in place precisely to insulate professional judgment from political pressure.

"A board that bypasses its own materials-review process to act on a political complaint hasn't exercised governance authority — it has abandoned it. Policy exists to protect the district from exactly this kind of ad hoc decision-making."

What Effective Boards Actually Do

The contrast between boards that govern well on this issue and boards that don't is not primarily ideological — it's procedural. High-functioning boards, when faced with organized pressure to remove materials, respond by directing the superintendent to ensure the district's review process is followed with fidelity. They ask: Was this title selected through our adopted process? Has a formal challenge been filed, and if so, has it moved through the review committee? What is the professional staff recommendation, and what evidence supports it? They do not schedule a board meeting to vote on individual titles before the review process has run its course.

In contrast, boards under political capture typically invert this sequence. A complaint arrives — often coordinated, often from outside the district — and within weeks the board places a removal vote on its agenda, frequently without any staff review and sometimes without the superintendent's recommendation. The board acts first; the process, if it happens at all, becomes retrospective justification. This is management-by-board at its most dysfunctional: elected officials substituting their own content judgments for the expertise of librarians, curriculum specialists, and educators.

The Legal Exposure Boards Create for Themselves

Beyond the governance dysfunction, boards that bypass their own procedures face significant legal risk. The First Amendment standard from Board of Education v. Pico (1982) remains contested but still operative: school boards may not remove books from libraries for the purpose of suppressing ideas, and when removal decisions lack procedural grounding, plaintiffs have a much easier time demonstrating impermissible motive. Courts have consistently shown more deference to removal decisions made through documented, content-neutral professional processes than to decisions that emerge from board member initiative, constituent pressure, or legislative mandate without individualized review.

The practical consequence is that districts win when they follow their policies and lose when they don't. A board that removes a book after a documented multi-stage review by a professional committee, applying consistent criteria, with a written recommendation to the board, is in a fundamentally different legal position than a board that votes to remove a title because three members read a complaint posted to a social media group. Both may produce the same outcome for that particular book. Only one of them is defensible.

The Accountability Gap at the Top

Superintendents often find themselves in an impossible position when boards demand rapid removal action. The ethical obligation is clear: the superintendent should insist that the district's adopted policy be followed, provide the board with an honest professional assessment of the materials in question, and recommend against any vote that bypasses established review. In practice, superintendents in politically heated districts frequently capitulate — removing books preemptively to avoid a board confrontation, or presenting removal recommendations to the board before any formal review process, because they calculate that keeping board members satisfied is more important than protecting institutional process.

This is a failure of professional courage, but it is also a failure of governance structure. Boards that have cultivated a culture where the superintendent is expected to deliver politically convenient outcomes rather than honest professional judgment have eroded one of the most important safeguards in public education. When a board member can call a superintendent on a Friday afternoon and expect a book to disappear from shelves by Monday without any formal process, the chain of professional accountability that makes districts function has already broken down — the book ban is just where it becomes visible.

What Boards Owe the Communities They Serve

There is nothing constitutionally or ethically impermissible about a school board deciding that certain materials are inappropriate for particular age groups, provided that decision flows from a principled, consistently applied, professionally grounded process. Communities have genuinely different values about what is age-appropriate, and boards elected by those communities are legitimate vehicles for expressing those values — within limits — through policy.

What boards owe their communities, however, is honesty about what they are actually doing. A board that removes books through a sham process, or with no process at all, is not exercising community values through legitimate governance — it is performing politics while wearing governance clothing. The harm is not only to the students who lose access to materials, or to the educators whose professional judgment is dismissed. The harm is to the institution itself. Every time a board substitutes political expediency for disciplined process, it erodes the trust and credibility that make board authority meaningful at all. And once that credibility is gone, it is very hard to get back — regardless of which books are on the shelves.
