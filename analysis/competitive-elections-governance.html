Do Competitive Elections Produce Better Boards? The Evidence Is More Complicated Than You Think

June 23, 2026|School Board Report Analysis
Back to Analysis

The democratic theory is seductive: contested elections force candidates to sharpen their positions, put their records before voters, and earn a mandate that gives them standing to govern. But when it comes to school boards, the evidence that competitive elections actually produce better governance is surprisingly thin — and in some cases, the opposite may be true.

What We Mean When We Say "Competitive"

Not all contested elections are created equal. A race with two candidates where one wins by thirty points is technically competitive but functionally a referendum. A race defined by a single hot-button issue — a curriculum controversy, a superintendent firing, a bond measure — may produce high engagement while selecting almost entirely on that single dimension, ignoring the full range of governance competencies a board actually needs.

When researchers and reformers talk about competitive elections improving board quality, they typically mean races where multiple well-informed, well-resourced candidates compete on a broad platform of governance priorities. That is a far narrower phenomenon than contested elections in general. And in most American school districts, that kind of genuinely competitive, substantive race is rare.

The Accountability Argument — and Its Limits

The strongest case for competitive elections rests on accountability. A board member who knows they can be voted out has an incentive to perform. They hold the superintendent to outcomes. They ask harder questions. They resist the drift toward passivity that characterizes boards where incumbents run unopposed for decades.

There is something to this. Districts where board members have faced credible opposition in recent cycles do tend to show higher rates of superintendent evaluation completion and more frequent adoption of explicit student outcome goals — two of the most visible markers of governance engagement. The prospect of having to explain your record to voters is a genuine motivator.

But accountability through elections is a blunt instrument. Voters rarely have access to the information needed to evaluate governance quality. They cannot easily distinguish a board member who asked probing questions in closed session from one who deferred to staff on every decision. What they can evaluate is visibility, personality, and position on whatever issue has recently inflamed the community. The accountability signal competitive elections send is often noise.

"Competitive elections select for campaigning skill. Effective governance requires an entirely different skill set — and districts that conflate the two often end up with boards that are very good at winning and very poor at governing."

When Competition Introduces Dysfunction

The more troubling pattern is what happens when competitive elections are driven not by genuine accountability but by organized interest groups with narrow agendas. In these races — and there have been hundreds of them in the past several years — candidates win on a single issue and arrive at the board table having made commitments to constituents that conflict with the board's actual governance role.

A board member who campaigned on "firing the superintendent" faces a structural problem the day after winning: the board's role is to evaluate the superintendent against adopted goals, not to execute a predetermined outcome. A board member who won on a platform of banning specific library books faces a similar tension — the governance mechanism for that concern is policy adoption, not directive management of library staff. When candidates campaign as if the board has operational authority it does not actually possess, winning creates a governance crisis, not a governance improvement.

The downstream effects are measurable. Boards that experience high turnover through contested elections show elevated rates of superintendent departure in the following twelve to eighteen months, more frequent policy reversals, and lower completion rates for multi-year strategic plans. The disruption is not always bad — sometimes it reflects legitimate course correction — but the pattern suggests that competitive elections often replace one set of governance problems with a different set.

The Uncontested Seat Problem Is Real, Too

None of this is an argument for uncontested elections. Boards where incumbents face no opposition tend to calcify. The same members serve for fifteen or twenty years, accumulating institutional knowledge but also institutional inertia. They stop asking basic questions because they assume they already know the answers. They develop relationships with district administrators that blur the line between oversight and collaboration. And when a superintendent is underperforming, they lack the standing — and sometimes the will — to act.

Districts with chronically uncontested seats also tend to have lower community awareness of what the board is actually doing. When no one is campaigning, no one is explaining. The board becomes a black box, which is its own form of accountability failure. The community that doesn't know what its board does cannot demand better governance from it.

What Actually Predicts Governance Quality

The research on board governance effectiveness consistently points to factors that are almost entirely orthogonal to how competitive the elections were. Board members who operate from a written set of governance norms — what many frameworks call "board operating principles" or a "governance culture document" — behave differently in meetings than those who rely on informal convention. Boards that maintain a clear distinction between their policy role and the superintendent's operational role produce better outcomes for students regardless of whether their members were elected in a three-way race or ran unopposed.

What matters is whether board members understand what the job actually is before they start doing it. That understanding is not a natural byproduct of winning an election. It has to be built deliberately — through orientation, ongoing training, and a board culture that reinforces the distinction between governance and management at every meeting. Competitive elections can bring in motivated new members who are receptive to that learning. They can also bring in members who arrived with such strong prior commitments that no training reaches them. The election result is not the determining variable.

The Governance Implication

For those who care about board quality — superintendents, state education agencies, governance advocates — the takeaway is not to suppress competitive elections or to celebrate them reflexively. It is to recognize that elections are the selection mechanism, not the governance mechanism. They determine who sits at the table; they do not determine what happens once everyone sits down.

The practical work is designing onboarding and governance structures that can absorb the disruption that competitive elections sometimes bring while capitalizing on the energy they can introduce. That means written governance agreements that persist across board transitions. It means superintendent evaluation frameworks that don't depend on institutional memory to function. It means board chairs who understand that their first job after a contested election cycle is integration, not consolidation of power.

Competitive elections are neither the problem nor the solution. They are a feature of democratic governance that produces radically different outcomes depending on what governance infrastructure exists to receive whoever wins. Districts that have built that infrastructure tend to absorb electoral turbulence. Districts that haven't tend to be defined by it.
