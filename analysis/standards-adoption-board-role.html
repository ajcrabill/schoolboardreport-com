Standards Adoption and the Board's Proper Role: Setting Policy, Not Choosing Programs

June 9, 2026|School Board Report Analysis
Back to Analysis

When a school board votes on academic standards, it is exercising one of its most consequential legitimate powers — defining what students in the district are expected to learn. When that same board starts debating which reading program to adopt or which math textbook series best aligns with those standards, it has crossed a line that undermines both effective governance and instructional quality.

The Distinction That Governs Everything

Governance clarity in education begins with a single, durable distinction: the board sets ends, and the superintendent manages means. Academic standards are ends — they describe the knowledge and skills students should demonstrate and the levels at which they should demonstrate them. Curriculum programs, instructional materials, and pedagogical approaches are means — the professional tools educators use to reach those ends. Boards belong firmly in the first category. When they drift into the second, they do not strengthen accountability; they compromise it.

This is not a semantic argument. It has direct consequences for student outcomes. Districts where boards routinely direct instructional decisions — favoring particular publishers, mandating specific programs, or requiring that certain approaches be used in classrooms — consistently struggle with coherence. Principals receive mixed signals. Teachers adapt to board preferences rather than student needs. And when outcomes disappoint, no one is clearly accountable because the lines of authority were never clean to begin with.

What Board-Level Standards Adoption Actually Looks Like

A board exercising its proper role in standards adoption does several things clearly and does them well. It reviews the standards framework being recommended, asks substantive questions about rigor and alignment with college and career readiness expectations, solicits community input on values embedded in the standards, and ultimately votes to adopt or reject the framework as policy. That vote carries real weight — it tells the superintendent what learning outcomes the community has authorized the district to pursue.

What that vote does not do is direct any particular instructional pathway. A board that adopts rigorous mathematics standards in grades K–8 has not thereby specified whether teachers should use a particular curriculum, follow a specific sequence of units, or employ any named pedagogical model. Those are implementation decisions. They belong to the superintendent and, through the superintendent, to the professional staff with the training and proximity to make them well.

"A board that micromanages curriculum selection does not hold the superintendent more accountable — it absorbs accountability that should stay with the superintendent, then has no one left to hold responsible when things go wrong."

Where Boards Routinely Go Wrong

The most common failure mode is not malicious — it is driven by genuine concern. Board members see low reading scores, hear from parents about confusing math homework, or attend a conference where a particular instructional approach is praised. They return to the boardroom with a conviction that the district needs to adopt a specific solution, and they begin using their governance authority to push for it. A board member asks at a public meeting why the district is not using a particular phonics program. Another seconds the concern. The superintendent — wanting to maintain board confidence — begins treating that program as a preferred option. The board has not formally voted, but the signal has been sent.

This informal pressure is often more corrosive than a formal vote would be because it lacks the accountability of a recorded decision. The superintendent cannot point to a board policy to explain a resource allocation. Staff cannot trace a curriculum shift to a transparent governance action. The board gets to feel it influenced outcomes without owning the consequences. And when the program does not produce the hoped-for gains, board members are free to distance themselves from it — or to repeat the cycle by pushing for yet another solution.

Standards as Policy Anchors, Not Political Objects

High-functioning boards treat adopted standards as durable policy anchors, not political objects to be revisited whenever community sentiment shifts. This matters because standards are only valuable if they are stable enough to drive coherent curriculum development, teacher training, and assessment alignment. Districts that reopen standards debates every two or three years — often driven by board members responding to advocacy campaigns — deprive themselves of the time horizon needed to see whether any instructional approach is actually working.

The board's legitimate ongoing role after adoption is to monitor outcomes against the standards it has set and to hold the superintendent accountable for progress. That means asking for disaggregated student achievement data, demanding honest analysis of where the district is falling short, and setting clear expectations for improvement timelines. It does not mean directing which curriculum the superintendent should purchase to address those gaps. If the board sets clear outcome expectations and the superintendent consistently fails to meet them, the board has the authority — and the obligation — to act on that. That is what accountability looks like in a well-governed district.

The Superintendent's Corresponding Obligation

Boards that stay in their lane can only do so if superintendents make it possible. A superintendent who presents standards adoption as a technical formality — asking the board to rubber-stamp whatever the state has prescribed without genuine engagement — is not serving the board's governance role well. Boards need enough substantive information to make a real decision: What do these standards require of students? How do they compare to prior expectations? What does research say about the standards framework's rigor and coherence? What feedback has the community provided?

Equally, a superintendent who allows board members to casually direct curriculum decisions without pushing back is not protecting the integrity of professional management. Part of the superintendent's role is to educate the board on the distinction between their governance function and the management function — not as a defense of turf, but as a defense of clarity. When that clarity erodes, it is almost always students who pay the price first, because the adults in the system are too busy navigating ambiguous authority to focus on what kids need to learn.

Building the Habit of Proper Governance

The boards that get this right do not stumble into it accidentally. They have established norms — often through explicit board self-evaluation processes and governance frameworks — that reinforce the ends-versus-means distinction consistently. When a board member raises a concern about a specific curriculum program in a board meeting, a well-functioning board chair redirects the conversation: "That's an instructional implementation question. Let's ask the superintendent to bring us a broader report on how the district is addressing this outcome gap, and we can hold that accountability conversation in the right frame." That kind of redirection is not dismissive. It is governance discipline.

Districts looking to strengthen this discipline should start with their board-superintendent governance agreements, sometimes called ends policies or board-superintendent compacts. These documents, when done well, explicitly define the boundary between policy and implementation and give the superintendent clear authority over instructional decisions. They also give board members a governance framework they can point to when community pressure pushes them toward overreach. The standards adoption process — recurring, visible, and consequential — is one of the best opportunities a district has to demonstrate that its governance culture is healthy. Boards that use it well build the trust and accountability infrastructure that makes everything else work better.
